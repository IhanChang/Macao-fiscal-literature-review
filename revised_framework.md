# Revised Theoretical Framework: Fiscal Reserves as Quasi-Monetary Buffer

## Core Innovation: Extending New Keynesian Framework with Quasi-Fiscal Operations

### The Key Insight

We can reconcile fiscal reserves supporting the peg with New Keynesian framework by treating fiscal reserve management as **quasi-fiscal operations** - institutional arrangements that blur fiscal-monetary boundaries in ways that are theoretically tractable and empirically common in small open economies.

**This is NOT a violation of New Keynesian principles** - it's an extension that recognizes second-best institutional arrangements in currency board systems.

## I. Theoretical Foundation: Sterilization-Based Framework

### 1.1 The Fiscal Surplus Sterilization Mechanism (Chan 2006)

**Key mechanism already documented in your literature:**

When Macao runs fiscal surplus:
- Government deposits increase at AMCM
- This **automatically sterilizes** money supply (removes liquidity from banking system)
- Creates contractionary pressure WITHOUT needing independent monetary policy

#### Detailed Mechanism Explanation (With Concrete Example)

**Scenario: Macao government collects MOP 10 billion in taxes but only spends MOP 8 billion, generating MOP 2 billion surplus**

**Step 1: Fiscal Surplus Generation**
- Taxpayers pay taxes from commercial bank accounts: MOP 10 billion
- Government pays civil servant salaries, public services, etc. from AMCM account: MOP 8 billion
- Net result: Government deposits at AMCM increase by MOP 2 billion

**Step 2: Banking System Liquidity Automatically Decreases (This is "Sterilization")**

Key insight: **Government deposits at AMCM are NOT part of money supply**

- When taxpayers pay taxes, money transfers from "commercial bank deposits" to "government deposits at AMCM"
- Commercial bank reserves at AMCM decrease by MOP 2 billion (because customer deposits decreased)
- This MOP 2 billion shifts from "circulating money" to "frozen government deposits"

**Analogy:** Like taking money out of a safe and locking it in a vault - less money circulating in the market

**Step 3: Interbank Market Liquidity Tightens**

- Commercial banks have less loanable funds
- Interbank lending rates rise (due to liquidity shortage)
- Real lending rates increase

**Step 4: Attract Capital Inflows**

- Macao interest rates rise → HKD capital flows into Macao for arbitrage
- Market demand increases to buy MOP
- Supports MOP/HKD exchange rate

**Critical Point: This is "Automatic" - AMCM Doesn't Need to Actively Operate**

- AMCM doesn't need to raise benchmark rates (it has no authority to do so)
- AMCM doesn't need to sell foreign exchange reserves
- Purely a "side effect" of fiscal surplus automatically generating monetary contraction

**NK-compatible interpretation:**
```
Fiscal Surplus → ↑Government Deposits at AMCM → ↓Commercial Bank Reserves → ↓Loanable Funds
                → ↑Interbank Rates → ↑Market Rates → Capital Inflow → Support Peg
```

#### Comparison with Traditional Monetary Policy

| Mechanism | Traditional Central Bank | Macao Fiscal Sterilization |
|-----------|-------------------------|---------------------------|
| Operator | Central bank active operations | Fiscal surplus automatic effect |
| Tool | Open market operations, rate adjustments | Government revenue-expenditure balance |
| Monetary Effect | CB buys/sells bonds to change money supply | Government deposit changes alter liquidity |
| Independence Requirement | Requires central bank independence | Not required - fiscal side effect |

**This is a "quantity-based adjustment mechanism"** that substitutes for interest rate policy - perfectly consistent with currency board constraints.

#### Why This is Particularly Important for Macao

1. **Macao runs persistent fiscal surpluses** (gaming tax revenue) → continuous automatic sterilization effect
2. **AMCM has no independent monetary policy tools** → fiscal sterilization becomes the only local adjustment mechanism
3. **Massive fiscal reserve scale** → means enormous "reversible sterilization capacity" (can release liquidity during stress)

#### Flow Diagram

```
【Normal Situation - No Fiscal Surplus】

Household/Business Deposits    Commercial Bank Reserves    AMCM Assets
   MOP 10bn   ────────→           MOP 10bn      ────────→  FX Reserves
                                                            Supporting Currency

Market Liquidity: Ample


【After Fiscal Surplus - Automatic Sterilization】

Household/Business Deposits    Commercial Bank Reserves    AMCM Assets
   MOP 8bn    ────────→           MOP 8bn       ────────→  FX Reserves

Government Deposits                                              ↓
   MOP 2bn    ────────→       (Locked in Gov Account) ────────→ (Frozen)

Market Liquidity: Tight → Interest Rates Rise → Capital Inflows → Support Peg


【Fiscal Deficit Release - Reverse Operation】

Household/Business Deposits    Commercial Bank Reserves    AMCM Assets
   MOP 12bn   ────────→           MOP 12bn      ────────→  FX Reserves

Government Deposits                                              ↑
   MOP 0bn    ────────→    (Released from Gov Account) ────────→ (Activated)

Market Liquidity: Loose → Interest Rates Fall → Stabilize Economy
```

**Key Understanding:**
- Fiscal reserves = "Frozen liquidity pool"
- Fiscal surplus = Automatically absorbs liquidity from market (sterilization)
- Fiscal deficit = Automatically releases liquidity to market (reverse sterilization)
- This process **requires NO active AMCM intervention** - it's a natural result of fiscal flows

### 1.2 Fiscal Reserves as "Sterilization Capacity"

**New interpretation:**

Fiscal reserves = **Accumulated sterilization capacity** that can be deployed countercyclically

- During boom: Fiscal surplus → sterilizes excess liquidity → prevents overheating
- During stress: Fiscal deficit → injects liquidity → prevents credit crunch
- The SIZE of fiscal reserves determines the **amplitude** of possible stabilization

**Why this matters for the peg:**
- Large fiscal reserves → large sterilization capacity → strong automatic stabilizer
- Markets know government can inject liquidity during stress WITHOUT depleting FX reserves
- This increases peg credibility even as FX reserves decline

### Channel 2: Portfolio Rebalancing Channel (Flow Effect)

**Original idea (from research plan):**

Fiscal reserves operate as an "investment fund" with a Strategic Asset Allocation (SAA) target. When capital outflow shocks occur, **no active policy decision is needed** - the mechanical requirement to maintain target weights **automatically** generates counter-cyclical stabilizing operations.

**New NK-compatible interpretation:**

This is NOT traditional monetary policy intervention, but **investment fund mechanical rebalancing that produces quasi-fiscal sterilization effects**:

#### **Detailed Mechanism (Black-Litterman Framework + Valuation Effect)**

**Step 1: Capital Outflow Shock and Valuation Effect**

Assume fiscal reserves have initial target weights:
```
Target portfolio w*: USD 30%, HKD 40%, RMB 30%
Initial market value: MOP 100bn each, total MOP 300bn
```

When capital outflow occurs (e.g., regional financial pressure):
- Private sector: Sells RMB and regional assets, buys USD/HKD (flight to safety)
- **Valuation Effect**:
  ```
  RMB assets depreciate: MOP 100bn → MOP 85bn (-15%)
  USD assets appreciate: MOP 100bn → MOP 105bn (+5%)
  HKD assets appreciate: MOP 100bn → MOP 103bn (+3%)
  ```

**Step 2: Passive Weight Drift**

Due to valuation changes, **before any active trading**, actual weights already deviate from target:
```
Post-shock actual weights w_pre:
USD: 105/(85+105+103) = 35.8% (target 30%, overweight by 5.8pp)
HKD: 103/293 = 35.2% (target 40%, underweight by 4.8pp)
RMB: 85/293 = 29.0% (target 30%, underweight by 1.0pp)
```

**Key point**: This deviation is **passive**, not from active decisions, but natural result of market valuation changes.

**Step 3: Mechanical Rebalancing Requirement**

Fiscal reserves have statutory or internal investment discipline requirements: **must maintain weights near target** (tracking error minimization)

To return to target weights, must execute rebalancing trades:
```
Required trades:
Sell USD: 5.8% × MOP 293bn = MOP 17bn
Buy HKD: Calculated adjustment needed
Buy RMB: 1% × MOP 293bn = MOP 2.9bn (or more, depending on precise calculation)
```

**Step 4: Stabilization Effect (Counter-cyclical Market Behavior)**

Fiscal reserve rebalancing trades generate **counter-cyclical effect** (Contrarian/Stabilizing Speculator):

```
Market behavior:      Private sector sells RMB → RMB depreciates
Fiscal reserve behavior:  Buys RMB (because weight too low, needs to restock)
```

**Key stabilization mechanism:**

1. **Provides RMB liquidity**
   - When private sector panic-sells RMB, fiscal reserves act as buyer
   - Reduces selling pressure in RMB market
   - Stabilizes RMB exchange rate

2. **Regional financial stability spillover**
   - RMB stabilizes → reduces regional financial panic
   - Regional financial stability → reduces pressure on Macao peg
   - Indirectly supports MOP-HKD currency peg

3. **Signaling effect**
   - Market observes "official" (Macao government through fiscal reserves) buying regional assets
   - Conveys signal that "crisis not severe"
   - Reduces panic capital outflows

#### **Why This is "Quasi-Fiscal Operations" NOT "Monetary Policy"?**

**Key distinction:**

| Feature | Traditional Monetary Policy Intervention | Fiscal Reserve Rebalancing |
|---------|----------------------------------------|---------------------------|
| **Decision Nature** | Active policy decision | **Passive** mechanical requirement |
| **Operation Goal** | Directly stabilize exchange rate | Maintain portfolio target weights |
| **Funding Source** | Central bank FX reserves | Government fiscal reserves (fiscal assets) |
| **Legal Constraint** | Requires central bank independence | **Not required** (this is asset management) |
| **Intervention Target** | Domestic currency (MOP) | Regional currencies (RMB etc.) |
| **Stabilization Path** | Direct | **Indirect** (through regional stability) |

**Why this fits NK framework:**
1. This is **fiscal operation** (government asset allocation), not monetary policy
2. Doesn't require central bank independence (just investment discipline)
3. But produces **quasi-monetary effects** through financial markets
4. This is precisely IMF's definition of "Quasi-Fiscal Operations"

#### **Overall Assessment of Mechanism Effectiveness**

**Theoretical advantages (why this mechanism is clever):**

1. ✓ **Auto-triggering**: No policy decision needed, valuation changes automatically trigger rebalancing needs
2. ✓ **Legal compliance**: Just maintaining investment discipline, doesn't violate statutory constraint of "not bearing monetary stability responsibility"
3. ✓ **Counter-cyclicality**: Naturally generates "buy in crisis" stabilizing behavior
4. ✓ **Scale advantage**: Fiscal reserves are massive (hundreds of billions), even small percentage adjustments have market impact

**Theoretical disadvantages (issues needing empirical testing):**

1. ⚠️ **Indirectness**: Stabilizes MOP through stabilizing RMB, transmission chain is long
2. ⚠️ **Time lag**: Portfolio adjustment takes time, may lag behind crisis peak
3. ⚠️ **Observability**: Can markets observe rebalancing operations in time?
4. ⚠️ **Systematicity**: Do markets believe this is rule-based rather than random?

## III. Formal Model Extension

### 3.1 Modified Currency Board Constraint

Traditional currency board:
$$M_t = \alpha \cdot FXR_t$$

Where $M$ = monetary base, $FXR$ = foreign exchange reserves, $\alpha$ = coverage ratio

**Extended constraint with fiscal sterilization:**

$$M_t^{effective} = \alpha \cdot FXR_t - \gamma \cdot (G_t - T_t)$$

Where:
- $M_t^{effective}$ = effective money supply available to private sector
- $(G_t - T_t)$ = fiscal balance (negative = surplus)
- $\gamma$ = sterilization coefficient

**Key implication:**
Even if $FXR_t$ declines, peg can be maintained if fiscal surplus increases (sterilizes liquidity)

### 3.2 Market Confidence Function

$$Pr(\text{Peg Abandonment})_t = h\left(\underbrace{FXR_t + \theta \cdot FR_t}_{\text{Consolidated Buffer}}, \underbrace{\Delta Portfolio_t}_{\text{Rebalancing Signal}}, X_t\right)$$

Where:
- $FR_t$ = fiscal reserves
- $\theta$ = market-perceived convertibility parameter (0 < $\theta$ < 1)
- $\Delta Portfolio_t$ = portfolio rebalancing flow
- $X_t$ = other controls (interest differential, RMB dynamics, etc.)

**Empirical strategy:**
- Test whether $\theta > 0$ using event studies (2015 anti-corruption, 2020 COVID)
- Test whether $\Delta Portfolio_t$ has significant short-run impact on credibility measures

```
                       = Total FR (Jan 2015) × 40.0%
```

**Advantages:**
- ✓ Simplest to implement
- ✓ Smooths out year-end noise

**Critical Problems:**
- ❌ **Loses timing information completely**
- ❌ **Severely biased for 2015-2016 period**

**2015-2016 Example showing the problem:**
```
Reality might be:
  Jan-Aug 2015: RMB still at 48.6%
  Sep-Dec 2015: RMB dropped to 31.4%

Using annual average (40.0% for all months):
  → Underestimates Jan-Aug holdings by 8.6pp
  → Overestimates Sep-Dec holdings by 8.6pp
  → Completely misses the TIMING of adjustment
```

**Fatal flaw for causal analysis:**
If we're testing "Does market stress in month t trigger rebalancing?", but we've artificially smoothed the data to show gradual change throughout the year, we cannot detect the actual response timing.

**Recommendation:** ❌ **Do NOT use this method** - too much information loss

---

#### **Method 2: Linear Interpolation Between Year-Ends (Better)**

**Formula:**
```
Weight_i,t = Weight_i,t-1_yearend + (Weight_i,t_yearend - Weight_i,t-1_yearend) × (month/12)
```

**Example for 2015:**
```
RMB weight in June 2015 = 48.6% + (31.4% - 48.6%) × (6/12)
                        = 48.6% - 8.6% = 40.0%

Then: RMB_amount_June2015 = Total_FR_June2015 × 40.0%
```

**Advantages:**
- ✓ Preserves total change magnitude over the year
- ✓ Allows for continuous variation
- ✓ Mathematically consistent (year-end matches actual data)

**Remaining Problems:**
- ⚠️ **Assumes linear adjustment** (reality may be stepwise)
- ⚠️ **Still imposes structure where we have no information**

**Example scenario where this fails:**
```
Reality: Adjustment happened all in August 2015 (811 reform)
  Jan-Jul 2015: RMB = 48.6%
  Aug-Dec 2015: RMB = 31.4%

Linear interpolation shows gradual monthly changes
  → Tests will spuriously find "adjustment spread over 12 months"
  → Misses the concentrated shock response
```

**Recommendation:** ⚠️ **Use with extreme caution**
- Label clearly as "interpolated, not observed"
- Do NOT over-interpret timing of effects
- Use for scale/magnitude analysis, not precise timing

#### **Method 3: Annual Data + Asset Class Decomposition (Recommended)**

**Key Insight:**

You have **monthly asset class data** (bonds, stocks, money market instruments). Use this to partially break the annual constraint!

**Two-Step Decomposition:**

**Step 1: Decompose currency weight changes into components**

```
Δ(USD_weight)_annual = [Asset Class Effect] + [Within-Class Currency Effect] + [Valuation Effect]
```

**Where:**
```
Asset Class Effect = Δ(Bond_share) × (USD_in_bonds - Overall_USD_weight)
                   + Δ(Stock_share) × (USD_in_stocks - Overall_USD_weight)
                   + Δ(MoneyMarket_share) × (USD_in_MM - Overall_USD_weight)
```

**Step 2: Use monthly asset class data to proxy monthly effects**

Since you observe asset class allocations monthly, you can construct:
```
Implied_USD_weight_t = Bond_share_t × [Assumed USD share in bonds]
                     + Stock_share_t × [Assumed USD share in stocks]
                     + MM_share_t × [Assumed USD share in MM]
```

**Critical assumptions needed:**
1. Currency distribution within each asset class (e.g., "bonds are 60% USD, 30% HKD, 10% RMB")
2. These within-class distributions are stable (or change slowly)

**How to estimate these:**
Use year-end data to back out implied within-class currency distributions:
```
At year-end 2014:
  Observed: USD = 28.9%, Bonds = 80%
  If bonds are X% USD: Need to solve system of equations
  Using all three currencies and three asset classes → 9 unknowns
  Using multiple year-ends → can estimate average distributions
```

**Advantages:**
- ✓ **Uses actual monthly variation** (in asset classes)
- ✓ Can potentially detect 2015-2016 "risk-off" shift (stocks→bonds)
- ✓ Provides economic interpretation ("USD rose because bond allocation rose, not currency shift per se")

**Remaining challenges:**
- Still requires assumptions about within-class currency distributions
- System may be under-identified without additional information

**Recommendation:** ✓ **This is the most robust approach**

**Concrete Implementation:**

```r
# Step 1: Estimate within-class currency shares using year-end data
# Solve: Observed_Currency_Weight = Σ(AssetClass_share × Currency_in_AssetClass)

# Step 2: Construct monthly implied weights
Monthly_USD_implied = Bonds_t × USD_in_bonds +
                      Stocks_t × USD_in_stocks +
                      MM_t × USD_in_MM

# Step 3: Decompose annual changes
Delta_USD_annual = [Bonds_effect] + [Stocks_effect] + [MM_effect] + [Residual]

# Where residual = changes in within-class currency distributions
```

**Testing strategy:**
```
Test 1: Does [Asset Class Effect] explain most of 2015-2016 RMB decline?
  → If yes: It's risk-off, not currency de-risking

Test 2: Do monthly asset class shifts respond to market stress?
  → Monthly_Δ(Bonds_share) = β × VIX + controls

Test 3: Is [Residual] (pure currency effect) counter-cyclical?
  → This tests the "pure rebalancing" hypothesis
```
